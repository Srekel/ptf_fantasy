Introduction to Pocket Task Force

A long time ago, three friends decided they wanted something to do during breaks between classes in university. They decided that a pen-and-paper role-playing-game would be fun, but classic RPGs have two features that made them unsuitable:

  1. They take a long time to play. [this might not necessarily be true, but we thought so at the time!] The sessions needed to be short; one to a few hours.
  2. One player needs to be the Game Master, leaving only two of us to actually play the game.

This didn't suit us, so naturally we decided to make our own game. The details of how it went down are forgotten, but as soon as we started, things fell into place pretty quickly. Since then, we've iterated on the concept and the rules many, many times. We've shown it to a lot of people (50-ish) and it's been a lot of fun seeing their reactions, watching them play the same missions we have, in completely different ways.

The idea for a fantasy version came up a few years after the original PTF, but we never got very far and we put PTF on a bit of a hold. But after many years, here it is. PTF: Fantasy Edition!

What's special about Pocket Task Force?

Pocket Task Force - PTF - is a pen-and-paper Role Playing Game with the following main Unique Selling Points:

  1. Everyone are Game Masters, and everyone plays a character (*).
  2. It's compact. The rules are and character sheet fit on a single sheet of A4. You can literally carry it in your pocket!
  3. Combat is deadly. An upfront battle will likely lead to the death of all of your characters.
  4. Pre-written lore is minimalistic. In PTF you make it up as you go along.

(*) Stricly speaking it's not necessary for everyone to play a character. We've sometimes had groups of six or more play, and then it can be more suitable for some players to just do the Game Master role. It works really well, as you will notice when playing the game. In fact, you'll likely find that more people are willing to not play a character than to not be a GM - it's just too much fun. :)

What's different about Pocket Task Force: Fantasy Edition?

The setting is the obvious part. The original Pocket Task Force is set in a near-future setting where the players play agents working for 'evil' corporations, doing less than legal missions.

In PTF: Fantasy Edition, you play as up-and-coming adventurers, taking jobs for... Well, you decide! While you'll face goblins and bandits and caravans and wizards and other things you might expect in a fantasy world, the gameplay of PTF is unlike anything you've tried before. :)

The most iconic part about PTF - that everyone is the Game Master, and The PTF Rule - is identical. All other rules are have been re-made from scratch to better suit a fantasy theme: character creation and development, dice rolling, combat, etc.

There's a lot more in PTF: Fantasy. Maybe this is due to fantasy being a richer theme than near-future-sci-fi, or maybe it's just because we're fleshing it out. This time, we are also making an effort to properly describe The PTF Rule and how to be multiple GMs at the same time. As a result, PTF: Fantasy no longer fits on a single piece of paper. To counteract that, there's now a web app that can compact your character sheet into something even smaller than before.

Rules for the Game Masters

In Pocket Task Force, everyone is the GM - Game Master - together. As you might expect, GMing works a little bit differently compared to traditional RPGs.

In case you've never played one of those (you totally should!), one player is the Game Master, and the other players role plays a personal character each. The players decide over their character actions but the GM is in charge of everything else: story, other characters (NPCs), explaining what the world is like, etc. In PTF, everyone is both GM and in control of a character.

If being a GM sounds difficult and intimidating, perhaps because you've had a great GM in another RPG before, don't worry. GMing in PTF is quite different, and very easy!

There is no hierarchy between the GMs. There's not a "main" GM that gets to decide when the group can't agree. It's not a democracy - you don't vote on what happens. PTF is governed by three things: Common sense, consensus, and the role of the die. Lets ignore the first part a bit and jump straight to how the actual mechanic works: The PTF Rule.

The PTF Rule

PTF is defined more than anything by its main rule, or mechanic. It is very simple, but very powerful - and fun!

To determine the state of the world, a character, an object, or to resolve what happens in a particular situation, this is what the GMs do:

  1. Talk and figure out the various reasonable options.
  2. Discuss their relative likelyhood/probability.
  3. When you've reached a consensus, arrange the options on a scale of a die of your choosing. A D20 is recommended due to it splitting up into easy-to-understand chunks of 5 percent.
  4. Roll the die!

Here's an example. Let's say your group wants to know what the weather is like. Maybe just for immersion, or maybe to know how hard it will be for a guard to spot you, or maybe your characters are following some tracks and one of the players suggests they may have been washed away by rain.

  1. You decide that the weather can be sunny, cloudy, or rainy.
  2. It was earlier decided that you are currently in a desert.
  3. You figure that means it's 80% chance of sun, 15% cloudy, 5% rain. On a D20 that's 1-16 = sun, 17-19 = clouds, 20 = rain.
  4. A roll of 1 could be interpreted as it's not only sunny, but scorching hot. 16 could be that there are some thin white clouds forming in the sky.

There are special rules for combat, magic, sneaking, talking, etc., and these may weave in The PTF Rule in various ways. You'll read about that under Player Mechanics.

Note that this is the rule in its entirety. There are some nuances that may not be immediately obvious, so we recommend you read the section called How to play PTF.

And, to be clear, this is the only rule for Game Mastering.

Rules for Player Characters

So how do you do the role playing aspects of the game? How do you create a character, and how do you resolve if she's successful in her attempts to do stuff? This shit right here, that's how.

Note that things in this section are very much subject to change.

Character creation

It's easy to get up and running in PTF. Creating a character is simple and fast - it shouldn't take more than ten minutes, even for a new player. However! If you create all your characters together, then you probably want to discuss amongst yourselves to find a good compisition. It's important to have spread out skills so you are ready for any kind of obstacle. This can significantly increase the time it takes to create characters, but of course, it's also a lot of fun. :)

Here's what you do:

  1. Pick a race.
  2. Pick two weapons.
  3. Pick an armor.
  4. Pick two perks.
  5. Pick one technique.
  6. Roll a D6 - the result is your Luck.

Character development

After a completed session, you gain a level.

  1. Increase you Health with an amount equal to your character's Strength. (Not sure about this)
  2. Add one perk.
  3. Add one technique.

Action Tests

  1. Rolls on actions performed by character are rolled with a dice pool - a number of D6 (six-sided dice).
  2. Before rolling, the character may Concentrate: Exchange one or more D6 for a D10.
  3. The result of a D6 die roll counts as following:
    • 1: Critical Fail (CF)
    • 2-3: Fail
    • 4-5: Success
    • 6: Critical Success (CS)
    In other words, equal chance of succeding or failing.
  4. The result of a D10 die roll counts as following:
    • 1-2: Fail
    • 3-10: Success
    In other words, 20% chance of Fail, 80% chance of Success. As you can see, using a D10 instead of a D6 increases the chance of a Success but removes any chance of getting a CS or CF.
  5. After rolling, the player can use one or more Fate Points to affect the result. When using an FP you are allowed to reroll one or more dice. You can do this as many times as you like, for as long as you have FPs.
  6. For each pair of CS and CF, remove both.
  7. For each pair of CF and Success, remove both.
  8. The following happens if there are any CSs left:
    • Each CS counts as two Successes.
    • Gain one Fate Point.
  9. And this happens if there are any CFs left.
    • 1 CF: Minor fumble.
    • 2 CF: Fumble.
    • 3 CF: Critical Fumble.
    • 4+ CF: Deadly Fumble.
    Of course it would be futile to try and list all of the things that can go wrong when fumbling with every skill in every circumstance. Therefore, use The PTF Rule to determine what happens.
  10. Finally, count up the number of total Successes. What happens with successes depends on what your character tried to do.

Attack

When attacking someone, you count the number of Successes and multiply that with the damage rating of the weapon,subtract the enemy's armor rating,

Working together

If two or more characters are trying to do the same thing, such as climb up some cliffs, they can help each other out. Any player who gets a CS can choose to give an extra D8 to a friend, instead of rolling on the CS table. This represents a skilled climber telling her partner where to grab and where to put the feet.

If two or more characters are working together to achieve a goal, such as pushing a big rock, they both roll dice, then count all the results as if it was one big dice pool. Round up the number of remaining CSs to be a multiple of the number of characters.

Nomenclature

  1. 1D6 means: A single six sided die.
  2. 2D6 means: A pair of six sided dice.
  3. 1D10 means: One ten sided die.
  4. AD6 means: Look at character's Agility. Roll that many D6.
  5. SD6, WD6, CD6 means: Same as AD6 but look at Strength, Wisdom, Charisma.
  6. AD6 * Strength for an action means: Roll AD6. Count the number of successes. Multiply that number with the character's Strength.
  7. AWD6 means: Add your Agility and Wisdom together. Roll that many D6s.

Combat

In combat, each character (player, allies, and enemies) has 2 actions per round. Each action can be used for either movement or an attack. Initiators get one extra action first round if they surprise the enemy.

To determine who goes first, roll an AWD6 Action Test for each combatant at the start of the combat. Highest value goes first.

When attacking, look at the Attack action of the character's equipped weapon. Roll the dice and calculate the total damage. Then subtract the enemy's armor rating from the damage. Subtract the damage from the total health. If that reaches 0, the enemy has died.

A Move Action allows you to move five meters. Using two move actions in one turn takes you roughly 20 meters.

When a character has less than half health left, she gets -1D6 on all rolls.

Sneaking

Combat is deadly in Pocket Task Force, so it's often a better idea to sneak past an enemy, or take them out without anyone else noticing. To sneak past someone, roll an AWD6 Action Test. The result needs to be higher than the enemy's Wisdom + modifiers. These modifiers are best left up to decide on the spot, but here are some guidelines:

  • In field of view and in light: 3
  • In field of view and in dark: 0
  • Within 5 meters on noisy ground (e.g. dry twigs): 3
  • Within 10 meters on soft ground (e.g. dirt): 0
  • Per enemy: 1
  • Enemy is on alert: 1
  • Enemy is looking for player: 1

Note that in some cases making sound doesn't play in, and in some cases visibility doesn't play in.

Techniques

A Technique is a way to amplify an action. Think of how Legolas jumps, stabs, and shoots in a continous motion.

Techniques can be combined, chained, and stacked. For example, if you have the chops for it, you could attack someone with two instances of The Piercer, then do a The Follow-up, and finish it off with The Chain Reaction, moving to a new enemy.

Technique costs are cumulative, so in the example above, the initial piercing attack would be made with -2D6, and the follow-up chain reaction attack -4D6.

Conversations with NPCs

When talking to an NPC in an RPG, it's common to actually play out the dialog: have the player and the GM talk to each other, acting as their characters. Because of how PTF GM-ing works, it is generally not how you do it, though you are of course free to do so if you wish.

Sometimes the player wants to know something and the NPC is completely fine with telling her. And sometimes, there will come a point in the conversation where the player and the NPC have different opinions or agendas or desires. The player then has to employ one or more actions to get what she wants.

For example, the player might want to pass a gate which is guarded. Or maybe she was caught red-handed somewhere she shouldn't have been, and need to lie to convince the NPC she isn't out of place. Or maybe she just needs to distract someone for a few seconds to allow one of the other players' characters to sneak past.

What this entails, from a player's point of view, is that she will just need to make an Action Test to see if she succeeds. Just like with combat, a single Success is enough.

From a GM perspective, there's a bit more work involved. Fun work! The role of the GM-ing (and to be clear - the player taking part in the conversation also participates in the GM-ing) is to envision the scene, consider the circumstances, and figure out what the NPC(s) think, and how everything affect modifiers for the Action Test.

At a rule level, there aren't any modifiers that apply specifically. While we could try and create tables for many different scenarios, that would 1) be a boring task 2) make players frequently say "Wait, I just have to look that up in the rules..." 3) remove a lot of the fun that comes with PTF, and 4) be quite impossible, as the list would always be incomplete.

There are a few guidelines however, to make it a bit easier to understand how to do it and roughly how difficult things are in PTF. Let's go through an example.

The player's character, a Halfling, is trying to get into an Elvish party, but there are two drunk guards posted outside the door. The player is disguised as a waiter. One of the guards has a high Hardcoreness value, 14.

A good rule of thumb is to remove 1D6 for each obstacle, and add one for each circumstance that benefits the player. In this case, the player would get:

  1. -1D6 because she's trying to convince more than one guard.
  2. -1D6, or even -2D6, for the fact that the guards don't expect a Halfling. Here it would matter if they were familiar with all of the staff or not, which of course depends on how big of a party it is and many other factors.
  3. The Hardcoreness of the guard yields another -1D6.
  4. Finally, the player would get +1D6 because the guards are drunk.
  5. (The disguise serves as a baseline, a minimal requirement for what's needed to get in, and therefore yields no bonus.)

When you're playing you'll find that this is a highly simplified example and you'll have lots of fun discussing the scene and what things could affect the player's chances.

So let's say, all-in-all, the player gets -2D6. She has a Charisma of 3, and a perk that gives her +1D6, so she needs to roll 2D6. If she succeeds with this roll, she will have convinced the guards that 1) she's really a waiter for the party 2) the fact that she happens to be a Halfling is A-ok 3) they should let her in.

Guidelines

  1. The D6 is fairly chunky. Removing or adding a whole D6 can affect the probability of success quite a lot, so consider that when deciding modifiers.
  2. For obstacles that are minor, it's probably not worth removing a whole D6. In the example above, if the player's disguise is the wrong size (maybe the NPC she stole the clothes from was a bit bigger), that might not be something two drunk guards would notice. However, if you have a couple or a few of these minor things, they can add up to a full -1D6.
  3. Large obstacles can, as you've seen, result in a -2D6 modifier. A good guideline for what consitutes "large" is to consider if it's something that really stands out, something you wouldn't expect, something that would make the NPC go "Huh, that's weird...". If the player tried to get into the party without any sort of disguise or good reason why, then that could be a good case for a hefty penalty.
  4. For Hardcoreness, here's an actual table (!). HC 1-5: +1D6. HC 11-15: -1D6. HC 16-20: -2D6.
  5. Generally it should be harder to convince more people, but it depends on the situation. If you're trying to lie to a bunch of people,it might be worth skipping the size of the audience, arguing that the ones who believe you may try to convince the ones who weren't so easily fooled. It'd be useless to try to come up with an all-encompassing rule for this. Thankfully, as GMs, you have full power to decide amongst yourselves how it should work out.

Magic

In PTF: Fantasy, there is no mana, fatigue, or other resource that limits the amount of spells you can cast. In theory, you could cast spells all day long, though in practice, it won't be an good strategy for completing an adventure successfully. There is one thing that limits your spellcasting: time.

Spells are charged. Charging a spell can take anywhere from just a single action up to a few minutes to cast. spell has a base time cost measured in actions. During the charging, the spell can fizzle out or critically fail. If you manage to keep charging it until it's done, the spell is cast.

A spell has a starting base cost, measured in Successes. For many spells, this is just a single Success. For other spells, it depends on what you are casting them on. In addition, spells have modifiers which allow you to improve the spell in various ways. Each modifier applies a -1D6 to your Action Tests and doubles the cost of casting the spell.

To cast a spell, you begin to charge it. You need to specify which modifiers to use and how many instances of each. Every action you do an Action Test based on your Wisdom. Every Success you get counts towards reaching the cost of the spell. If you reach the cost, the spell is successfully cast.

If a whole turn passes with zero Successes made towards charging the spell, it fizzles out with no effect.

If, during an Action Test, you get a Critical Fail, the spell is turned loose before it's ready. Depending on the number of ones you got, and depending on how close you were to the charge goal, the worse it gets. The effect of this is resolved by using The PTF Rule.

Casting a spell in PTF: Fantasy is a bit messy. You need to use one hand to keep the charging alive, and it is easily seen by someone who observes you, and heard by someone within earshot. The effect is tied to the spell you use, making appropriate sounds and conjuring up images of whatever it is you're trying to cast.

How to play PTF

You can always fall back on The PTF Rule

The PTF Rule can always be used as an alternative to any other mechanic, or as a fallback when the general rules don't apply to a particular circumstance. Maybe you're trying to convince a whole room of people that the local vampire they've been harassing is, in fact, not evil at all, and that a mutual cooperation would work out great for everyone. Instead of rolling a test for every person to see how they react, you can figure out the odds for how many people believe you and how many start reaching for their pitchforks, and resolve that with a single roll.

Lookup tables and common target values

You might wonder what some standard target values for some things are when it comes to The PTF Rule. Like, what are the possible states and probabilities for a door? Well, a door can generally be open, closed and locked. But you don't need us to tell you that! It could also be magically locked, slightly ajar, unlockable only by two keys getting turned at the same time, there could be a small door for cats inside the door, and the cat-door's state doesn't have to match the regular door's state. All of these options and how to resolve the openness of the door can be found in Table 34C - Doors.

Just kidding. There are no tables for The PTF Rule. As you can tell, the options are endless, and there's only one way to find out. Talk about it. Is it the door to a king's treasury room? Does the king have a cat? Ask yourself as many questions as possible to ensure that you're not missing some important detail. Then use common sense and decide what the probabilities of the options are - we like to think of it in terms of percentage - then map it to a D20, and roll the die!

How not to use The PTF Rule

PTF gives the players a lot of power. It is easy to abuse it, and to use it in the wrong way. Obviously, players are allowed to play PTF however they want, and we encourage you to play it in the way suit your preferences and play styles the best. What follows are recommendations for how we feel is the best way to get the most out of the game, based on how we have played the game and on how others have played it.

Making the game too easy

You might be asking: But if the players are all game masters, they can just decide that all enemies are hit by lightning and that doors are unlocked and that chests are filled to the brim with... right?

Technically, yes. PTF does require a degree of self control in the players. But so do all co-op games. You could easily just remove half of the diseases in the board game Pandemic, but it wouldn't be much fun. Or, you know, maybe you do like playing that way, and that's fine too. Just know that the designers of the game came up with a ruleset in which they tried to maximize the fun, so you should probably try and keep to the rules unless you have good reason to break them.

Making the game too simple

Here's something we've noticed in some players that don't get PTF.

Ok, so I'm standing by a door. I'm going to try to open it.
Wait, we should check if it's locked or unlocked to see if you can do that.
Sure. Well it's fifty-fifty I guess?
*Rolls D20*
15. It's unlocked.

If you play PTF like this, you're gonna have a bad time. We've watched players go into detail about the shape and angle of a roof and what type of roof tiles it has and how old the house was, just to be able to decide if they could skyglide to it and land safely from a nearby mountain. They had a much better time. You need to figure out where your preferred level of detail lies, but we promise that more detail and higher fidelity will almost always lead to more interesting choices and a more immersive experience.

Making the game too actiony

For a couple of missions, during the development of PTF, we thought it sounded fun to make it feel more like an action movie. People doing somersaults and throwing themselves through windows, guards being oblivios and stupid, etc.

That sounds pretty good, and if you can make it work for you - by all means, go ahead. But for us, it didn't work so well. Here's why: It is hard to do it without falling for the "making PTF too easy" trap. If you are playing the action heroes, and you're facing the villains, then it's all but predetermined that you will complete the mission without any substantial problems. If you can always justify something that is towards your advantage with the argument that "That's what would happen in an action movie!", then you are going to do that. The problem then becomes that all tension is lost, and tension is one of greatest thing you can feel when playing a game like PTF.

How to actually use The PTF Rule

When PTF is at it's best, you will feel like your group is on the brink of failure, stuck in a bad situation you put yourself in, possibly facing death for all your characters, despite being all-knowing, powerful GMs. How is that possible? How do you create such situations, full of tension?

Use common sense

When deciding upon the options and their chances for The PTF Rule, approach it from a direction of common sense, realism, and believabilty. If you do this, you will not feel like you cheated your way to a win. Did the guard forget to look around, then choose to turn left instead of right, thereby missing you hiding in the darkness? Well, you came up with reasonable percentages and rolled the die, and it came up in your favor. Totally fair! Had you instead tried to make up an excuse for why she would just turn around. [todo]

Stay away from tropes

Similarly to the advice regarding action movies above, but more generally speaking: Tropes are bad in PTF. Maybe you're playing noir detectives in PTF: Fantasy. Obviously, it always rains, right? That's one less thing that can go wrong or right, and instead just... is. Following such ideas will only lead to streamlining a game where you want things to be able to be unpredictable, even in the face of apparent total control. You want conflict and tension and if everything is clear because it follows a theme... [todo]

Secrets

Here's probably the trickiest subject with the whole everyone's the GM thing: How do you manage things that are secret to the players' characters? Secret codes, NPC out-of-sight actions, PCs fumbling and believing they managed to trick the guard but she's going to ring the alarm as soon as the player leaves, etc. There are a few options here, but we'll wait with talking about them until a later chapter on advanced PTFing!
